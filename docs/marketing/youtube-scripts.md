- How pure is it? (99%? 80%? 50%?)
- What are the impurities? (inert filler or contamination?)
- Is it degraded? (broken peptide chains?)"

---

**[0:45-2:15] SECTION 1: WHAT IS HPLC?**

[Animated diagram: Laboratory setup]

"HPLC stands for High-Performance Liquid Chromatography. Sounds scary. It's not."

[Show: Simplified diagram]

"Here's how it works:

Step 1: You inject your peptide sample into a machine.
Step 2: The sample flows through a column packed with tiny beads.
Step 3: Different compounds stick to the beads for different amounts of time.
Step 4: As compounds come out (elute), a detector measures their absorption.
Step 5: A computer draws a graph showing what came out and when.

Result: A chromatogram. A graph that shows what's in your sample."

[Animated demonstration of flow]

"Why does this work? Because peptides have different chemical properties. BPC-157 sticks to the beads for exactly X minutes. Degraded BPC-157 (broken chains) sticks for Y minutes. Completely different compound sticks for Z minutes.

Same sample, but different compounds take different routes out. When they come out at different times, you see different peaks on the graph."

[Show: Example chromatogram with annotations]

---

**[2:15-3:30] SECTION 2: READING A CHROMATOGRAM**

[Show: Real chromatogram, annotated]

"Let's read an actual chromatogram. This is what a pure BPC-157 looks like."

[Point to X-axis]

"X-axis: Time in minutes. The column is flowing. Compounds are moving through."

[Point to Y-axis]

"Y-axis: Absorbance. How much light the compound is absorbing as it elutes. Higher peak = more compound."

[Point to peaks]

"Main peak: This is BPC-157. It comes out at 20.1 minutes. Area under the peak tells you concentration."

[Point to baseline]

"Baseline: This is noise. Ideally it's <5% of your main peak height."

[Show: Pure vs. contaminated chromatogram side-by-side]

"Pure BPC-157: One clean peak. Nothing else."

"Contaminated or degraded: Multiple peaks around the expected time. Those are broken chains, isomers, or other compounds."

[Text overlay: "More peaks = More problems"]

"When you see multiple peaks:
- Could be degradation (age, heat damage)
- Could be contamination (impure starting material)
- Could be wrong compound entirely (vendor mistake)"

---

**[3:30-4:45] SECTION 3: WHAT VENDORS SHOULD PROVIDE**

[Show: Good COA vs. minimal COA comparison]

"Here's what a real vendor provides with HPLC testing:"

[Good COA example]

"A real HPLC report includes:
1. **Method detail** — What equipment, column type, temperature, flow rate
2. **Chromatogram image** — The actual graph
3. **Retention time** — When your compound came out (20.1 min, not 15.3 min)
4. **Purity calculation** — Main peak area ÷ total area = purity %
5. **Impurity breakdown** — What's the other 1-2%?
6. **Reference standard** — Comparison to a known standard
7. **Lab accreditation** — ISO 17025 certification
8. **Batch number** — Matches your product"
